Methodology — Raw vs Settled DQ rate, and the maturity caveat

What this tab shows

Raw DQ rate = Disqualified leads ÷ All leads created in the period. This is what your CRM dashboard shows and what most stakeholders expect.

Important caveat for recent months — maturity bias

Sales takes time to work leads. A lead created today won't be DQ'd today — it'll spend time in Working or Nurturing before sales decides. So for the current month, a chunk of leads are still "in flight" and haven't been judged yet. Because raw DQ rate divides by all leads (including those in-flight ones), the current month always looks artificially low — even though many of those in-flight leads will eventually be DQ'd.

As of Apr 29, around 43% of Apr 2026 leads are still in flight. Expect the Apr raw DQ rate to drift up over the next 30–60 days as those leads settle.

Alternative: Settled DQ rate

Settled DQ rate = Disqualified leads ÷ Settled leads only, where "settled" means a lead has reached either Disqualified or Converted status — i.e., sales has finished judging it. Leads still in Working, Nurturing, Assigned, Engaged, or New are excluded from both numerator and denominator.

The settled rate is more apples-to-apples across months because every month uses the same denominator definition. For trend questions ("are DQ rates getting worse?") settled is more accurate. We're showing raw because that's what your CRM and stakeholders use, but if you want a true month-over-month comparison, ask for the settled view and we can compute it.

How the DOW × DOM pacing model works

Why not just use linear pacing?

A simple "we're 29/30 days through the month so we should be at 96.7%" approach is misleading. Inbound MQLs cluster heavily on weekdays (especially Tue–Wed), barely arrive on weekends, and within a month tend to peak around days 18–20. Linear pacing would flag us as "behind" every Monday morning and "ahead" every Friday afternoon — driven by calendar artefacts, not real performance. The DOW × DOM model corrects for this.

The model in one line

Every day of the target month gets a multiplier based on (a) its day-of-week and (b) its day-of-month, normalised so the multipliers across the month sum to the number of days. Cumulative pace at any point in the month = monthly forecast × sum of multipliers through that day ÷ total month multiplier.

Day-of-Week factor (fitted from history)

For every day in our historical window (Jan 2024 → end of last completed month) we computed its share of that month's MQL/S2/$ total, then averaged that lift by Mon/Tue/…/Sun. Numbers above 1.00 are above-average days; below 1.00 are below-average.

S2 and Pipeline $ have their own DOW curves (fit on S2-date counts and S2-Piped-iACV totals respectively) — typically more concentrated on weekdays since S2s require sales action.

Day-of-Month factor

Same approach but grouped by day-of-month (1–31). Captures within-month rhythms — for ROLLER, that's a clear ramp into days 18–20 and a softer end-of-month. With ~28 months of history each calendar position has 27–28 observations.

Combined per-day weight (current month: Apr 2026)

For each day d in the target month: raw[d] = DOW[dow(d)] × DOM[dom(d)], then normalised so the month's weights sum to 30. An average day has weight ≈ 1.0; a Tuesday near day 19 might be ~1.6×; a Saturday near day 31 might be ~0.5×.

Expected MTD (paced)

Cumulative weight through Apr 29 (day 29) for MQL = 96.3%, S2 = 92.5%, $ = 90.1% of the month-end total. So Expected MTD = monthly forecast × this share. Linear pacing on the same day would predict 96.7% — the difference is what the model is correcting for.

QTD and YTD

Completed months contribute 100% of their monthly forecast (we have full-month actuals to compare). Only the current partial month uses the paced calculation. So YTD expected = (Jan + Feb + Mar full forecasts) + (April forecast × paced share).

What this won't capture

Single-day spikes from one-off campaigns or large enterprise deal imports — the model produces a smooth expected curve. When actuals exceed forecast on a particular day due to such a spike, the dashboard will (correctly) show "ahead of pace" but the cause is exogenous, not a sustained trend.
