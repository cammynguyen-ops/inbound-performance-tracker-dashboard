How the DOW × DOM pacing model works

Why not just use linear pacing?

A simple "we're 28/30 days through the month so we should be at 93.3%" approach is misleading. Inbound MQLs cluster heavily on weekdays (especially Tue–Wed), barely arrive on weekends, and within a month tend to peak around days 18–20. Linear pacing would flag us as "behind" every Monday morning and "ahead" every Friday afternoon — driven by calendar artefacts, not real performance. The DOW × DOM model corrects for this.

The model in one line

Every day of the target month gets a multiplier based on (a) its day-of-week and (b) its day-of-month, normalised so the multipliers across the month sum to the number of days. Cumulative pace at any point in the month = monthly forecast × sum of multipliers through that day ÷ total month multiplier.

Day-of-Week factor (fitted from history)

For every day in our historical window (Jan 2024 → end of last completed month) we computed its share of that month's MQL/S2/$ total, then averaged that lift by Mon/Tue/…/Sun. Numbers above 1.00 are above-average days; below 1.00 are below-average.

MQL fit:

MonTueWedThuFriSatSun
1.12×1.17×1.15×1.06×0.94×0.76×0.79×

S2 and Pipeline $ have their own DOW curves (fit on S2-date counts and S2-Piped-iACV totals respectively) — typically more concentrated on weekdays since S2s require sales action.

Day-of-Month factor

Same approach but grouped by day-of-month (1–31). Captures within-month rhythms — for ROLLER, that's a clear ramp into days 18–20 and a softer end-of-month. With ~28 months of history each calendar position has 27–28 observations.

Combined per-day weight (current month: Apr 2026)

For each day d in the target month: raw[d] = DOW[dow(d)] × DOM[dom(d)], then normalised so the month's weights sum to 30. An average day has weight ≈ 1.0; a Tuesday near day 19 might be ~1.6×; a Saturday near day 31 might be ~0.5×.

Expected MTD (paced)

Cumulative weight through Apr 28 (day 28) for MQL = 92.5%, S2 = 86.1%, $ = 83.8% of the month-end total. So Expected MTD = monthly forecast × this share. Linear pacing on the same day would predict 93.3% — the difference is what the model is correcting for.

QTD and YTD

Completed months contribute 100% of their monthly forecast (we have full-month actuals to compare). Only the current partial month uses the paced calculation. So YTD expected = (Jan + Feb + Mar full forecasts) + (April forecast × paced share).

What this won't capture

Single-day spikes from one-off campaigns or large enterprise deal imports — the model produces a smooth expected curve. When actuals exceed forecast on a particular day due to such a spike, the dashboard will (correctly) show "ahead of pace" but the cause is exogenous, not a sustained trend.
